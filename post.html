WHAT SQL TAUGHT ME ABOUT HOW THE WORLD WORKS

PLAYER: AKSHAT TIWARI
DATE: JAN 2026
READ TIME: 5 MIN
XP REWARD: +250 XP

Every dataset tells a story. It took me months of writing queries before I realized that SQL wasn't just a technical skill — it was a way of thinking.

The quality of your insights is limited by the quality of your questions.

When you write a query, you can't be vague. You have to define exactly what you want, from where, under what conditions. That discipline changes how you think about everything else.

// LESSON 01: EVERYTHING IS RELATIONAL

The world doesn't exist in isolated silos. Customers connect to orders. Orders connect to products. SQL forces you to model these relationships explicitly, and once you start seeing everything as connected — you can't unsee it.

SELECT
  customers.name,
  COUNT(orders.id) AS total_orders,
  SUM(orders.value) AS lifetime_value
FROM customers
LEFT JOIN orders ON customers.id = orders.customer_id
GROUP BY customers.name
ORDER BY lifetime_value DESC;

This isn't just code. It's a question: who are my most valuable customers? The moment you can ask that and get an answer in seconds — something shifts in your brain.

// LESSON 02: AGGREGATION IS POWER

Individual data points are noise. Patterns are signal. GROUP BY is one of the most powerful ideas I've encountered — stop looking at individual rows, start looking at what they mean at scale.

This is true everywhere. You don't understand traffic by watching one car. You understand it by watching thousands over time and finding the patterns.

// LESSON 03: QUESTIONS FIRST, QUERIES SECOND

The biggest mistake beginners make is opening a query editor before knowing what they're looking for. Always start with the question in plain English. Then translate it to SQL.

This applies far beyond databases. Clarity of thought precedes clarity of output — in code, in writing, in conversation.
